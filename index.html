    • What is PIGSR?

    The Preferences Instrument for Genomic Secondary Results (PIGSR) is a brief, validated tool to allow adults undergoing genomic testing to record their preferences about getting incidental or secondary results.

    PIGSR is available for download under a Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 International license.

    How do I use PIGSR to elicit preferences?

    PIGSR can be completed on paper, or on a computer or mobile device. Head to the getting started section to download PIGSR in PDF and Word formats. Coming soon, we will have XML format that you can import into REDCap. We encourage you to adapt PIGSR to fit your needs.

    PIGSR is designed to be completed by your patients or research participants without direct guidance from study personnel or healthcare providers. You can introduce PIGSR to your patients or research participants by saying something like:

    “The genetic test we are performing today may provide information beyond [primary reason for testing]. It is possible that this genetic information may someday be useful for your health or your child’s health, and we are happy to provide it to you if you want it. Please fill out this questionnaire so we can find out what genetic information you would like to receive from us.”

    How do I apply PIGSR to genomic results?

    According to focus groups we conducted [link to reference], patients and research participants do not want their clinicians to receive genomic results that they will then withhold from the patient or participant. Based on this finding, it is better to apply preferences about which genomic results patients and participant desire before the results are reported to providers. PIGSR is designed to lend structure to patient and participant preferences so they can be applied programmatically during the process of analyzing raw genomic data.

    Since the human genome contains 20,000 genes, it is practically impossible for a patient to record his or her preference for receiving every possible genomic result. However, PIGSR is designed so that patterns of preferences can be used to extrapolate just 15 questions to a wide range of variants and conditions. Filtering of genomic results based on PIGSR responses involves two steps: (1) Identifying the pattern of preferences and (2) using this pattern to select genes that should be included in the analytic plan.

    Meet the PIGSR Team

    Kyle Brothers, MD, PhD is an Assistant Professor in the Department of Pediatrics at the University of Louisville, where he is also affiliated with the Institute for Bioethics, Health Policy, and Law. Dr. Brothers’ research focuses on policy and ethics in human genetics research and the translation of genomic technologies into clinical care. His current work on PIGSR is funded by the National Human Genome Research Institute’s Clinical Sequencing Exploratory Research program. Dr. Brothers is a practicing pediatrician and experienced clinical ethicist. Dr. Brothers received his MD from the University Of Louisville School Of Medicine. He completed his residency training and chief residency in Pediatrics at Vanderbilt Children’s Hospital, and his PhD in Ethics and Society at Vanderbilt University.

    Ellen Wright Clayton, MD, JD, is Craig-Weaver Professor of Pediatrics, Professor of Law, and Professor of Health Policy in the Center for Biomedical Ethics and Society at Vanderbilt University and Medical Center. She has long been involved in working to ensure the optimal conduct of genetics research and its incorporation into clinical care and has been happily collaborating with Dr. Brothers for several years. She is a member of the National Academy of Medicine and a Fellow of the American Academy for the Advancement of Science.

    Carla Rich, MA is a Research Coordinator in the Kosair Charities Pediatric Clinical Research Unit in the Department of Pediatrics at the University of Louisville. As a graduate student, Ms. Rich worked with Susan E. Kelly, PhD on a two year NIH funded study which explored rural Kentucky families’ experiences in accessing healthcare and services for their children diagnosed with a genetic condition. More recently, she has collaborated on federally funded studies sponsored by the Kent School of Social Work at the University of Louisville as well as state and community funded studies sponsored by the Survey Research Center at the University of Louisville. Ms. Rich is experienced with in-depth personal interviewing and survey research methodologies—both by phone and in-person. Ms. Rich completed her Masters degree in Sociology at the University of Louisville where she happily took the lead in creating projects to fund student research.
